1. Introduction to X-Ray Radiation

What are X-Rays?

X-rays are electromagnetic radiation with photon energies typically ranging from 100 eV to 100 keV, corresponding to wavelengths of approximately 0.01 to 10 nm. In laboratory XRD and XRF instrumentation, characteristic X-rays are produced through electron bombardment of metal target anodes (commonly Cu, Mo, Cr, or Ag).

Production Mechanism

When high-energy electrons (accelerated through potentials of 20-60 kV) strike a metal target, two types of X-radiation are produced:

1. Bremsstrahlung (Braking Radiation):
  • Continuous spectrum resulting from electron deceleration
  • Energy range: 0 to maximum electron kinetic energy (eV)
  • Intensity proportional to Z² (atomic number squared) and electron current
  • Contributes to background radiation and scattered dose
2. Characteristic Radiation:
  • Discrete spectral lines specific to target material
  • Produced when inner-shell electrons are ejected and outer electrons transition
  • Common laboratory sources:
    • Cu Kα: 8.04 keV (λ = 1.54 Å) - most common for XRD
    • Mo Kα: 17.4 keV (λ = 0.71 Å) - used for single crystal XRD
    • Cr Kα: 5.41 keV (λ = 2.29 Å)
    • Ag Kα: 22.1 keV (λ = 0.56 Å)

X-Ray Properties Relevant to Safety

Penetrating Power:

The linear attenuation coefficient (μ) determines penetration depth according to: I(x) = I₀ × e(-μx)

For Cu Kα (8.04 keV) radiation:

  • Air: μ ≈ 0.034 cm⁻¹ (penetrates meters)
  • Soft tissue: μ ≈ 0.5 cm⁻¹
  • Lead: μ ≈ 130 cm⁻¹
  • Aluminum: μ ≈ 2.8 cm⁻¹
Ionizing Radiation Characteristics:

X-rays interact with matter through three primary mechanisms:

  • Photoelectric Absorption (dominant at laboratory X-ray energies): Photon transfers all energy to bound electron; responsible for most biological damage
  • Compton Scattering (significant at higher energies): Partial energy transfer to loosely bound electrons; important for scatter radiation fields
  • Rayleigh (Coherent) Scattering: Elastic scattering with no energy loss; dominant mechanism in XRD
Biological Effects:

X-rays deposit energy in tissue through ionization cascades:

  • Direct DNA strand breaks (double-strand breaks particularly hazardous)
  • Indirect damage via free radical formation (OH•, H•, e⁻aq)
  • Cellular repair mechanisms may be overwhelmed at high dose rates
  • Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE) ≈ 1 for X-rays
Invisibility and Detection:

X-rays cannot be detected by human senses, requiring instrumentation:

  • Ion chamber survey meters (recommended for dose rate measurements)
  • Geiger-Müller detectors (qualitative detection, energy-dependent response)
  • Thermoluminescent dosimeters (TLDs) for personnel monitoring
  • Film badges (historical method, largely replaced by TLDs)
Key Point: Laboratory X-ray sources produce photon energies in the 5-25 keV range, where photoelectric absorption in tissue is maximum, resulting in high biological effectiveness per unit energy deposited.

2. XRD and XRF Instrumentation

X-Ray Diffraction (XRD) Systems

Fundamental Principle:

XRD exploits Bragg's Law to analyze crystalline structure: nλ = 2d sinθ

Where: n = diffraction order, λ = X-ray wavelength, d = interplanar spacing, θ = incident angle

Powder XRD Configuration:
  • Sample form: Polycrystalline powder, thin films, or bulk materials
  • Geometry: Bragg-Brentano (θ-θ or θ-2θ) most common
  • Typical 2θ range: 5° to 90°
  • Applications: Phase identification, crystallinity, lattice parameters
  • Radiation hazards: Primary beam exposure during sample loading, scattered radiation from sample, diffracted beams at specific 2θ angles
Single Crystal XRD Configuration:
  • Sample form: Single crystal (typical size 0.1-0.5 mm)
  • Geometry: Four-circle goniometer or area detector systems
  • Applications: Complete structure determination, absolute configuration
  • Enhanced radiation hazards: Crystal centering requires beam access, goniometer manipulation near beam path, extended measurement times (hours to days), higher power sources often used

X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) Systems

Fundamental Principle:

Primary X-rays eject inner-shell electrons; characteristic fluorescent X-rays are emitted as outer electrons fill vacancies. Fluorescent energy is element-specific, enabling compositional analysis.

Energy-Dispersive XRF (EDXRF):
  • Detector: Solid-state (Si(Li), SiPIN, or SDD)
  • Energy resolution: 130-150 eV at 5.9 keV
  • Detection range: Na (Z=11) to U (Z=92)
  • Configuration: Often enclosed sample chamber
Handheld/Portable XRF:
  • EXTREME HAZARD: Open-beam configuration
  • Typical operating: 40-50 kV at 10-200 μA
  • Primary beam fully accessible when not in contact with sample
  • Shutter failure can result in unshielded exposure
  • Requires specialized safety protocols

X-Ray Sources and Operating Parameters

Sealed X-Ray Tubes (Most Common):
  • Construction: Evacuated glass or ceramic envelope
  • Cathode: Tungsten filament (thermionic emission)
  • Anode: Cu, Mo, Cr, or Ag target (water or air cooled)
  • Typical operational life: 2,000-10,000 hours
  • Operating parameters: Voltage: 20-60 kV, Current: 5-60 mA, Power: 0.5-3.0 kW
Dose Rate Characteristics:

For a typical 40 kV, 40 mA Cu tube:

  • Exit port intensity: ~5 × 10⁵ R/hr
  • Annual occupational limit (5 rem) delivered in: 0.036 seconds
  • Lethal dose (500 rem) delivered in: 3.6 seconds
Inverse Square Law Application:

Dose Rate (r) = Dose Rate (r₀) × (r₀/r)²

Example calculation:

  • If dose rate at 1 cm = 10,000 R/hr
  • Then dose rate at 10 cm = 10,000 × (1/10)² = 100 R/hr
  • And dose rate at 100 cm = 10,000 × (1/100)² = 1 R/hr

Quantitative Hazard Assessment

Direct Beam Exposure Scenario:

Assume operator's hand enters primary beam for 1 second:

  • Tube: 45 kV, 40 mA Cu source
  • Primary beam intensity: 5 × 10⁵ R/hr
  • Exposure distance: 5 cm from focal spot
  • Corrected intensity: 5 × 10⁵ × (1/5)² = 2 × 10⁴ R/hr
  • Dose in 1 second: 2 × 10⁴ R/hr × (1/3600 hr) ≈ 5.6 R
  • Result: This exceeds annual whole-body limit (5 rem) in 1 second
Scatter Radiation:

At 90° scattering angle, 50 cm from sample:

  • Scattered intensity ≈ 0.1% of primary beam
  • If primary = 10⁵ R/hr, scattered = 100 R/hr at sample
  • At 50 cm: 100 × (10/50)² = 4 R/hr (still extremely hazardous)
CRITICAL WARNING: Even "low power" laboratory X-ray instruments operating at 1-3 kW can deliver fatal doses in seconds if safety interlocks are bypassed or defeated. There is no safe direct beam exposure scenario.

3. Biological Effects of X-Ray Radiation

Mechanisms of Radiation Damage

Energy Deposition and Ionization:

When X-rays interact with biological tissue, energy is deposited through ionization events. For laboratory X-ray energies (5-25 keV):

  • Linear Energy Transfer (LET) ≈ 2-3 keV/μm in tissue
  • Energy required to create ion pair in tissue: ~33 eV
  • For 8 keV photon (Cu Kα): ~240 ion pairs per photon interaction
  • Ionization density creates clusters of molecular damage within ~2 nm radius
Direct vs. Indirect Damage:

Direct Damage (~30% of total):

  • Ionization of critical molecules (DNA, RNA, proteins)
  • Direct strand breaks in DNA double helix
  • Base damage and cross-linking

Indirect Damage (~70% of total):

  • Radiolysis of water molecules: H₂O → H₂O⁺ + e⁻
  • Formation of free radicals: Hydroxyl radical (OH•), Hydrogen radical (H•), Hydrated electron (e⁻aq)
  • Free radicals diffuse and damage nearby molecules
  • Chain reactions amplify initial damage
DNA Damage Types:
  • Single-strand breaks (SSB): ~1000 per Gy per cell - usually repaired efficiently
  • Double-strand breaks (DSB): ~40 per Gy per cell - more difficult to repair, can lead to chromosomal aberrations
  • Base damage: ~2000 per Gy per cell - can cause point mutations if misrepaired
  • DNA-protein cross-links: Complex lesions difficult to repair

Deterministic Effects (Tissue Reactions)

Deterministic effects exhibit threshold doses and severity increases with dose.

Skin Effects (most relevant for hand/extremity exposure):

Acute Dose Thresholds (single exposure):

  • Transient erythema: 2 Gy (200 rad)
  • Main erythema: 6 Gy (600 rad)
  • Dry desquamation: 10 Gy (1000 rad)
  • Moist desquamation: 15 Gy (1500 rad)
  • Blister formation: 20 Gy (2000 rad)
  • Ulceration: 25 Gy (2500 rad)
  • Necrosis: 30+ Gy (3000+ rad)

Timeline:

  • 0-24 hours: Immediate erythema (transient, >10 Gy)
  • 7-14 days: Main erythematous reaction
  • 14-28 days: Dry desquamation, epilation
  • 21-35 days: Moist desquamation (severe exposure)
  • >6 weeks: Late effects, possible necrosis
Example Calculation:

Hand exposed to primary beam (10⁵ R/hr) for 0.5 seconds:

  • Dose = 10⁵ × (0.5/3600) ≈ 14 R ≈ 14 rad ≈ 0.14 Gy
  • Below erythema threshold but significant injury if repeated

Hand exposed for 3 seconds:

  • Dose = 10⁵ × (3/3600) ≈ 83 R ≈ 83 rad ≈ 0.83 Gy
  • Approaches transient erythema threshold - visible skin reddening likely
Eye Lens Effects:
  • Threshold for detectable opacities: 0.5 Gy (50 rad) acute
  • Threshold for vision-impairing cataract: 2-10 Gy (200-1000 rad) acute
  • Latency period: 6 months to several years
  • Annual occupational limit (lens): 15 rem (150 mSv)
Hematopoietic Effects (whole-body exposure):
  • Detectable blood changes: 0.25 Gy (25 rad)
  • Mild radiation sickness: 1 Gy (100 rad)
  • Severe radiation sickness: 4-6 Gy (400-600 rad)
  • LD₅₀/₆₀ (lethal dose, 50% mortality, 60 days): 3-5 Gy without treatment

Stochastic Effects (Cancer and Genetic Effects)

Stochastic effects have no threshold; probability increases with dose.

Cancer Risk Estimates (ICRP Publication 103):
  • Nominal risk coefficient: 5.5% per Sv for whole population
  • For occupational exposure: 4.1% per Sv (adult workers)
  • Fatal cancer: 4.0% per Sv
  • Non-fatal cancer: 0.8% per Sv
  • Severe hereditary effects: 0.2% per Sv
Risk Calculation Example:

Whole-body exposure: 100 mrem (1 mSv)

  • Excess lifetime cancer risk: 1 mSv × 0.055/Sv = 5.5 × 10⁻⁵
  • (approximately 1 in 18,000 chance of fatal cancer)
Latency Periods:
  • Leukemia: 2-10 years (peak at 7 years)
  • Solid tumors: 10-60 years (peak at 40+ years for some cancers)
  • Hereditary effects: appear in offspring

ALARA Principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)

ALARA is not just regulatory compliance but optimization:

  • Dose limits: Regulatory maximum (5 rem/year occupational)
  • Dose constraints: Administrative control levels (typically 10% of limit)
  • ALARA goal: Achieve doses well below constraints
Key Point: For properly functioning, enclosed XRD/XRF systems, operator doses should be effectively zero (<10 mrem/year). Any measurable dose indicates potential safety system degradation requiring immediate investigation.

4. Radiation Dose and Units

Understanding Radiation Measurements

Traditional Units:
  • Roentgen (R): Measure of exposure in air (ionization)
    • 1 R = 2.58 × 10⁻⁴ coulombs/kg air
    • Only applies to X-rays and gamma rays in air
  • Rad (Radiation Absorbed Dose): Energy deposited in tissue
    • 1 rad = 100 ergs/g = 0.01 J/kg
    • Applies to all radiation types
    • For X-rays in soft tissue: 1 R ≈ 1 rad (approximately)
  • Rem (Roentgen Equivalent Man): Biological effect of radiation
    • rem = rad × QF (Quality Factor)
    • QF = 1 for X-rays and gamma rays
    • For X-rays: 1 rad = 1 rem
SI Units:
  • Gray (Gy): SI unit for absorbed dose
    • 1 Gy = 1 J/kg = 100 rad
  • Sievert (Sv): SI unit for dose equivalent
    • 1 Sv = 100 rem
    • Sv = Gy × WR (Radiation Weighting Factor)
    • WR = 1 for X-rays
    • For X-rays: 1 Gy = 1 Sv
Effective Dose:

Effective Dose (E) = Σ (wT × HT)

Where: wT = tissue weighting factor, HT = equivalent dose to tissue T

Example: 50 rem to hands

  • Effective dose = 50 rem × 0.01 (skin wT) = 0.5 rem
  • (This is why extremity limits are higher than whole-body limits)
Dose Rate vs. Total Dose:
  • Dose Rate: Amount of dose per unit time (rem/hr, mSv/hr)
  • Total Dose: Integrated dose over exposure time (rem, mSv)
  • Formula: Total Dose = Dose Rate × Time

Example: Working in 10 mR/hr field for 3 hours

  • Total dose = 10 mR/hr × 3 hr = 30 mR

5. Safety Features of X-Ray Equipment

Engineered Safety Controls - Detailed Technical Specifications

Primary Shielding Design:

Half-Value Layers for Common Materials (Cu Kα, 8.04 keV):

Material HVL (mm) Tenth-Value Layer (mm)
Lead 0.03 0.10
Steel 0.35 1.16
Aluminum 2.8 9.3
Concrete 18 60

Typical Enclosure Shielding:

  • Cabinet XRD systems: 1-2 mm lead equivalent
  • XRF analyzers: 1-3 mm lead equivalent
  • Rotating anode systems: 3-6 mm lead equivalent

Safety Interlock Systems - Redundancy and Fail-Safe Design

Interlock Functional Requirements (ANSI N43.2):
  • Redundant Interlock Requirement: Minimum of TWO independent interlocks required for personnel safety
  • Each interlock must independently prevent X-ray generation
  • Interlocks must be fail-safe (power loss = beam off)
  • Single-point failure shall not allow hazardous condition
Types of Interlocks:
  • Hard-wired Interlocks (Primary):
    • Direct electrical circuit interruption
    • Independent of software control
    • Break high-voltage or filament circuit
    • Response time: <10 milliseconds
  • Monitored Interlocks:
    • Position switches with status monitoring
    • PLC or control system verification
    • Alarm generation on fault
  • Trapped-key Interlocks:
    • Physical key exchange system
    • Beam operation key cannot be removed unless enclosure locked
    • Provides positive physical assurance

Beam Shutters and Attenuators

  • Primary Shutter Design: Material: Lead (5-10 mm typical) or tungsten
  • Fail-safe position: CLOSED (beam blocked)
  • Status indication: Physical position sensors (2 independent)
  • Automatic shutter closure on: Enclosure opening, system fault, emergency stop, power failure

Warning and Indication Systems

Visual Warning Requirements:
  • Primary X-Ray Warning Light:
    • Color: RED (per ANSI N43.2)
    • Label: "X-RAYS ON" or "RADIATION"
    • Location: Visible from all access points
    • Activation: Whenever high voltage is applied to tube
Warning Labels and Placards:
  • Required Radiation Symbol (Trefoil): Magenta or purple on yellow background
  • Standard Caution Label: "CAUTION: X-RAY RADIATION - THIS EQUIPMENT PRODUCES X-RAYS WHEN ENERGIZED"
  • Supplementary Information: Maximum tube voltage and current, RSO contact, emergency procedures

Administrative Safety Controls

Pre-Operational Safety Checklist:
  • ☐ Completed required training and authorized by RSO
  • ☐ Verified all interlocks functional (test each door/panel)
  • ☐ Confirmed warning lights operational
  • ☐ Inspected enclosure for damage or modifications
  • ☐ Verified shutter closed before opening enclosure
  • ☐ Reviewed instrument-specific procedures
  • ☐ Confirmed no unauthorized modifications
  • ☐ Checked radiation survey meter available and calibrated
Interlock Testing Protocol:

Frequency:

  • Daily: Visual inspection of interlocks and warning lights
  • Quarterly: Functional test of each interlock
  • Annually: Comprehensive safety inspection by qualified personnel

Quarterly Interlock Test Procedure:

  1. Verify X-ray tube is off and cooled
  2. Close all enclosure doors/panels
  3. Attempt to energize X-ray tube
  4. Verify warning light illuminates
  5. Open each door/panel sequentially
  6. Verify X-ray generation immediately ceases
  7. Verify warning light extinguishes
  8. Repeat for all interlocks
  9. Document results in maintenance log
  10. Report any anomalies to RSO immediately

Acceptance Criteria:

  • X-ray generation must cease within 10 milliseconds of interlock opening
  • No residual X-ray production (verify with survey meter)
  • Warning lights must accurately reflect X-ray status
  • Beam cannot be re-energized until interlock restored
CRITICAL POINT: Safety interlocks are the PRIMARY defense against X-ray exposure. Any modification, bypass, or defeat of interlocks transforms a safe instrument into an immediately life-threatening hazard. Under NO circumstances is interlock defeat justified, even temporarily for troubleshooting or alignment. Interlock tampering may result in immediate loss of authorization, regulatory violations and fines, criminal liability in case of injury, and institutional liability.

6. Safe Operating Procedures

Before Operating X-Ray Equipment

  • Complete required safety training
  • Verify all safety interlocks are functional
  • Check that warning lights are operational
  • Ensure enclosures and shielding are intact
  • Review instrument-specific procedures
  • Verify you are authorized to operate the equipment

During Operation

  • Never open enclosures while X-rays are on
  • Keep hands and body parts away from the primary beam path
  • Use beam stops and shutters when not actively measuring
  • Monitor warning lights and indicators
  • Follow sample loading/unloading procedures exactly
  • Never attempt to view the X-ray beam

After Operation

  • Properly shut down the X-ray generator
  • Close beam shutters
  • Remove and secure keys if applicable
  • Log usage in instrument logbook
  • Report any equipment malfunctions immediately
Best Practice: Always assume the X-ray beam is on unless you have personally verified it is off.

7. Hazard Recognition and Control

Three Fundamental Principles of Radiation Protection

PRINCIPLE 1: TIME

Minimize exposure duration to reduce total dose received.

Dose-Time Relationship: Total Dose = Dose Rate × Time

Example Quantitative Analysis:

  • Scenario: Sample alignment near scatter source
  • Dose rate at work position: 50 mR/hr
  • Time to receive annual limit (5 rem = 5000 mR): 100 hours
  • Time to receive quarterly limit (1250 mR): 25 hours

By reducing time from 30 min to 15 min per alignment:

  • Dose reduction: 50% immediately
  • Annual dose if weekly alignments (30 min): 1300 mR
  • Annual dose if weekly alignments (15 min): 650 mR

Practical Time Minimization Strategies:

  • Work Planning: Prepare tools and materials before approaching radiation area; review procedures thoroughly beforehand
  • Efficiency Optimization: Automate repetitive operations; use programmable sample changers
  • Remote Operation: Computer-controlled sample positioning; video monitoring for sample visualization
  • Dose Budgeting: Allocate time for dose-intensive operations; monitor accumulated dose vs. time estimates
PRINCIPLE 2: DISTANCE

Maximize distance from radiation source to reduce exposure.

Inverse Square Law: I(r) = I₀ × (r₀/r)²

Example 1: Primary Beam Intensity vs. Distance

Primary beam at 1 cm from focus: 10⁶ R/hr

Distance (cm) Intensity (R/hr) Annual Limit Time
1 10⁶ 0.018 sec
2 2.5 × 10⁵ 0.072 sec
5 4 × 10⁴ 0.45 sec
10 10⁴ 1.8 sec
50 400 45 sec
100 100 3 min

Key Point: Doubling distance → dose rate reduced to 25%; 10× distance → dose rate reduced to 1%

Practical Distance Maximization:

  • Workspace Design: Control console separated from instrument; minimum approach distance marked on floor
  • Sample Handling: Use long-handled tools for sample mounting; never lean over enclosure during operation
  • Scatter Minimization: Proper collimation reduces scatter production; position body perpendicular to beam
PRINCIPLE 3: SHIELDING

Interpose absorbing material between source and operator.

Exponential Attenuation: I(x) = I₀ × e(-μx)

Example: Lead Shielding for Cu Kα

  • Initial intensity: 1000 R/hr
  • Shield thickness: 0.5 mm lead
  • Result: Essentially complete attenuation
  • For 99% reduction: Only 0.043 mm lead needed
  • Practical shield: 0.1 mm lead provides >99.99% reduction

Practical Shielding Applications:

  • Permanent Shielding: Instrument enclosure, beam tube shielding - never modify or remove
  • Portable Shielding: Lead brick barriers, lead-acrylic panels for viewing
  • Shielding Integrity Verification: Annual inspection; radiation surveys detect gaps

Hazard Recognition - Identifying Dangerous Situations

CATEGORY 1: Equipment Malfunctions

Interlock Failure Indicators:

  • X-ray warning light remains on when door opened
  • Ability to energize X-rays with enclosure open
  • Intermittent interlock function
  • Visible damage to interlock switches or actuators
  • Warning light fails to illuminate when X-rays generated

Immediate Actions:

  1. Do NOT open enclosure if warning light on
  2. Turn off X-ray generator at main power
  3. Post "DO NOT OPERATE" sign
  4. Immediately notify RSO
  5. Do not resume operations until repaired and tested
CATEGORY 2: Procedural Violations

High-Risk Behaviors:

  • Operating equipment without completed training
  • Bypassing or defeating interlocks "temporarily"
  • Working on equipment with power on
  • Alignment procedures without proper tools/shielding
  • Using equipment with known malfunctions
  • Unauthorized modifications or repairs
CATEGORY 3: Beam Alignment Hazards

Alignment is inherently hazardous and requires special procedures:

  • Controls: Reduce tube power to minimum; install beam attenuators; use remote viewing (cameras, fluorescent screens)
  • Two-person rule: One monitoring, one working
  • Use radiation survey meter continuously
  • NEVER: Place any body part in primary beam path; attempt to "see" the X-ray beam; use hands to check beam position

Practical Hazard Case Studies

CASE STUDY 1: Hand Exposure During Sample Loading

Scenario: Operator loading powder sample, becomes distracted, forgets X-ray generator is energized. Interlock has been bypassed (unauthorized). Hand enters primary beam for 2 seconds.

Analysis:

  • Tube: 40 kV, 40 mA (Cu Kα)
  • Primary beam intensity: ~5 × 10⁵ R/hr
  • Distance: 5 cm from focal spot
  • Corrected intensity: 2 × 10⁴ R/hr
  • Dose = 2 × 10⁴ R/hr × (2/3600 hr) = 11.1 R ≈ 11 Gy to hand

Predicted Effects:

  • Main erythema in 7-14 days
  • Possible dry desquamation
  • Moist desquamation likely
  • Pain, swelling, possible blistering
  • Medical follow-up required

Lessons: NEVER bypass interlocks; always verify generator is off before opening; interlock defeat transformed safe instrument to serious injury

CASE STUDY 2: Chronic Low-Level Exposure from Shielding Defect

Scenario: XRF system develops crack in lead shielding (1 mm wide, 2 cm long). Operator works at console 50 cm from crack for 4 hours/day. Condition persists for 6 months before quarterly survey detects it.

Analysis:

  • Dose rate at operator: 40 mR/hr
  • Daily exposure: 40 mR/hr × 4 hr = 160 mR/day
  • 6-month exposure: 160 × 5 × 26 = 20,800 mR ≈ 21 rem
  • This exceeds annual occupational limit (5 rem) by factor of 4

Lessons: Regular inspection and surveys detect degradation; small defects can produce significant exposures; importance of trending dose data

Remember: You cannot see, hear, or feel X-rays. Always rely on safety systems and procedures. The difference between safe operation and serious injury is measured in milliseconds for laboratory X-ray systems.

8. Emergency Procedures

Suspected Overexposure

If you suspect you or someone else has been exposed to X-rays:

  1. Immediately turn off the X-ray generator
  2. Move away from the equipment
  3. Notify your supervisor immediately
  4. Contact the Radiation Safety Officer (RSO)
  5. Seek medical attention if advised
  6. Document the incident in detail
  7. Do not resume operations until cleared by RSO

Equipment Malfunction

  • Shut down the system immediately
  • Post "DO NOT OPERATE" sign on equipment
  • Notify supervisor and RSO
  • Document the malfunction
  • Do not attempt repairs unless authorized

Interlock Failure

If an interlock fails to shut off X-rays when opened:

  • Do not open the enclosure further
  • Use emergency stop button if available
  • Turn off power at the main switch
  • Evacuate the area
  • Post warning signs
  • Immediately report to RSO
